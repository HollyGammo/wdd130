Site Name

Snakes of Pennsylvania

Elevator Pitch - Purpose

Snakes are fascinating creatures that many people are terrified of. They have forked tongues, scales, and fangs. These reptiles are often misunderstood and feared because people are not well informed about the nature of these animals. In Pennsylvania, there are 19 native species of snakes. 16 non-venomous species and 3 venomous. Knowing important facts about these snakes can replace fear with information and curiosity. If you encounter one of the venomous snakes in Pennsylvania, it can be helpful to know what to do in case of a bite. This site will introduces all Pennsylvania snakes, show the anatomy of a snake and snake facts, and then explain what to do in case of a snake bite.

Target Audience

Snakes of PA will be a site for the people living in the state of Pennsylvania. It will offer photos and information to anyone who wants to know about the different species of snakes in the area they live in. People who spend a lot of time outdoors, gardening, camping, hiking, etc. will benefit for this information.

Anatomy of a Snake
  • Scales - Snakes bodies are covered with scales. Without this protective armor snakes could not move over rough or hot surfaces like tree bark, rocks, and hot desert sand. Their scales are also nearly waterproof and help to keep the water out. Rough belly scales allow the snake to keep their grip on rough branches and to push off of surfaces when they need to move.
  • Shedding - When a snake is ready to shed it’s eyes get cloudy and turn a blue-ish color which makes it temporarily blinded. It will then rub up against a rough surface like a rock to break the skin near the mouth and then begin to crawl and slide out of it's skin. Snakes shed their skin as they grow bigger.
  • Forked Tongue - A snake uses its tongue to take in scent particles, effectively using its tongue to smell. These scent particles are flicked by the tongue up against the Jacobson’s organ on the roof of the mouth. The two tips of the tongue allow the snake to find the location of what it has smelled. Snakes use their forked tongue to smell and find food, find a mate, and to avoid predators.
  • Teeth and Fangs -A snakes teeth are angled backwards so they can grab their food and walk it back into their mouths. Although most snakes have teeth, four rows on the top and two on the bottom, not all snakes have fangs. Only venomous snakes have fangs. Fangs are sharp, long, hollow or grooved teeth that are connected to a small gland in the snake's head behind its eyes. These glands produce venom which is injected when a snake bites.
  • Tail Rattle - A rattlesnake has a tail made of keratin, the same hard substance as your fingernails. The keratin is arranged in a chain of interlocking rings, which are hooked together by tiny grooves along their edge. When vibrated, the rattle creates a hissing sound that warns off potential predators. It is an extremely effective and highly evolved predator-avoidance system.
  • Cold Blooded - Snakes are cold-blooded animals that lack the ability to generate body heat internally. These animals must rely entirely on external sources to regulate their body temperature, both to stay warm and to avoid becoming overheated.
  • How They Eat - Snakes are carnivorous. That means that they eat other animals. Snakes d not have the right kind of teeth to chew their food so they must eat their catch whole. Some snakes inject venom to paralyze their prey while others constrict. All snakes jaws are structured in such a way that it allows the mouth to open wider than their own body in order to swallow their prey whole.
  • Eyes - Most snakes have poor eyesight. Vipers, pythons and boas have holes on their faces called pit organs, which contain a membrane that can detect infrared radiation from warm bodies up to one meter away. At night, the pit organs allow snakes to 'see' an image of their prey — as an infrared camera does — giving them a unique extra sense.
What TO DO For a Snake Bite
  • Stay calm.
  • Call 911 immediately to be evaluated.
  • Take off anything constricting the area, such as a ring or watch.
  • Elevate the bite. Position the affected area at or above heart level.
  • Make note of the time the bite occured and outline the swollen and red area with a marker. This will aid the doctors with treating the bite.
  • Go to the emergency room as soon as possible.
What NOT TO DO For a Snake Bite
  • DON'T apply a tourniquet or a constriction band.
  • DON'T apply ice, it can cause tissue damage.
  • DON'T apply heat.
  • DON'T cut the affected area and attempt to suck the venom out. This can cause infection.
  • DON'T apply any type of lotions or ointments.
