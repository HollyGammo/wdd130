Snakes

Snakes are fascinating creatures that many people are terrified of. They have forked tongues, scales, and fangs. These reptiles are often misunderstood and feared because people are not well informed about the nature of these animals. In Pennsylvania, there are 21 native species of snakes. 18 non-venomous species and 3 venomous. Knowing important facts about these snakes can replace fear with information and curiosity. If you encounter one of the venomous snakes in Pennsylvania, it can be helpful to know what to do in case of a bite. This site will introduce all Pennsylvania snakes, show the anatomy of a snake, and then explain what to do in case of a snake bite.

Venomous Snakes of Pennsylvania

There are three venomous snake species in Pennsylvania, the Copperhead, the Timber Rattle Snake, and the Massasauga Rattle Snake. Some key physical differences between venomous and non-venomous snakes help with identification. Native venomous snakes in Pennsylvania have an indentation or pit on each side of the head between the eye and nostril, a vertically elliptical eye pupil resembling that of a cat, and a single row of scales on the underside of the tail. Nonvenomous snakes in Pennsylvania do not possess facial pits, have round eye pupils and a double row of scales on the underside of the tail. The Copperhead Snake

Copperhead snakes are commonly seen in Pennsylvania and are found throughout the state. Of the three venomous snakes in the state, they're also the most likely to bite. However, their venom is relatively mild, and their bites are rarely fatal for humans. These snakes get their name from their copper-red heads. These snakes are pit vipers, like rattlesnakes and water moccasins. Pit vipers have heat-sensory pits between eye and nostril on each side of head, which are able to detect minute differences in temperatures so that the snakes can accurately strike the source of heat, which is often potential prey.

The Timber Rattlesnake

Timber rattlesnakes are large pit-vipers and have a distinctive rattle on the tail and a black, velvety colored tail. Because of the distinctive black tail, these snakes are often called Velvet tails. Black bands run down the entire body to the tail in a chevron pattern. These venomous snakes are found throughout the state, with heavy populations in the mid region.

The Massasauga Rattlesnake

The eastern massasauga rattlesnake has been listed as a threatened species under the Endangered Species Act. Massasaugas are small snakes with thick bodies, heart-shaped heads and vertical pupils. The average length of an adult is about 2 feet. Adult massasaugas are gray or light brown with large, light-edged chocolate brown blotches on the back and smaller blotches on the sides.

Non-Venomous Snakes

The other 18 species of snakes native to Pennsylvania are non-venomous. These snakes tend to be found near cover such as fallen logs, brush piles, rock walls, abandoned house foundations or rock ledges, where snakes may be resting or lying in wait for prey.
